# Exporting Cosmetics to Japan: The Complete A-to-Z Guide

*Last updated: May 2026 — by Cleo Labs*

Japan is the **second-largest cosmetics market in the world** by retail value (~$32 B in 2025), with the highest per-capita beauty spend on the planet. Japanese consumers are also among the most ingredient-literate and brand-loyal in any developed market. For a foreign cosmetics brand, **Japan is a slow, complex, high-reward market**. The single most important fact: you cannot ship one bottle to a Japanese consumer without first solving the **importer / marketing authorization holder problem**.

This guide walks through the actual rules — the **Pharmaceutical and Medical Device Act (PMD Act)**, the **MHLW** notification system, **quasi-drug classification**, Japanese-language labelling, and the importer / distributor setup that every foreign brand must navigate.

## The fundamental rule

Cosmetic products in Japan are regulated by the **Pharmaceutical and Medical Device Act** (薬機法, *yakkihō*), enforced by the **Ministry of Health, Labour and Welfare (MHLW)** and the **Pharmaceuticals and Medical Devices Agency (PMDA)**.

Under the Act, two product categories matter for foreign brands:

1. **Cosmetics (化粧品 *keshōhin*)** — general cosmetic products
2. **Quasi-drugs (医薬部外品 *iyakubugaihin*)** — products with claims like whitening, anti-acne, anti-perspirant, hair growth, anti-dandruff, anti-hair loss

A product becomes a **quasi-drug** based on its **claims** and certain **active ingredients**. If you claim "whitening" in Japan, the product is a quasi-drug. Quasi-drugs need **PMDA pre-market approval** that takes **6–18 months** and **~¥3–8M (€18K–€50K)** per product.

If you stay in the **cosmetic** category, the path is faster and cheaper but requires:

- A **Japan-based Marketing Authorization Holder (MAH)**, or *Sōseihantenkō* (総製造販売業者)
- A **Manufacturing Licence Holder** (or licensed importer)
- **MHLW notification** before first sale
- **Japanese-language labelling** per regulation
- **GMP** compliance (Japan's domestic version, closely aligned with ISO 22716)

## The MAH — the key actor

The **Marketing Authorization Holder** is the legal entity in Japan responsible for product safety, quality, and post-market surveillance. The MAH must be **licensed by the Prefectural Governor** (Type 2 cosmetics licence) and **established in Japan**.

You cannot **be your own MAH** unless you have a Japanese entity with the right licence. Options:

- Partner with an **established Japanese distributor** that holds a Type 2 MAH licence
- Hire a **MAH service company** (specialized agency that acts as MAH for foreign brands)
- Set up your **own Japanese subsidiary** with MAH licence (long, expensive, 6–12 months + ¥10M capital)

MAH service companies typically charge **¥80,000–¥200,000/mo** (€500–€1,300/mo) plus **¥30,000–¥80,000/SKU/yr**, plus **start-up costs of ¥150,000–¥400,000**.

## The importer

You also need a **licensed importer**. This can be:

- The same entity as the MAH (most common)
- A separate Type 2 manufacturing licence holder
- A distributor that handles customs clearance and bonded storage on your behalf

The importer is the **first commercial recipient** in Japan and is responsible for clearing customs, ensuring the product matches the MHLW notification, and storing it under proper conditions.

## MHLW notification

For each cosmetic SKU sold in Japan, the MAH files a **manufacturing/sales notification** to MHLW via the **Prefectural Health Department**. Contents:

- Product name (Japanese characters + English)
- Type of cosmetic per MHLW category list (skincare, makeup, fragrance, hair, oral, etc.)
- Full quantitative + qualitative ingredient list, using **Japanese Cosmetic Ingredient Codex (JCID)** names
- Manufacturing site (with country and licence info)
- Importer details
- Storage conditions

**Notification is per SKU**. There is no central public-facing portal like CPNP — submissions go through the prefecture where the MAH is licensed.

## Ingredient rules — the positive/negative lists

Japan operates a **mixed system**:

- **Negative list** (Article 4 PMD Act, **MHLW Notification No. 331/2000**): substances **banned** in cosmetics
- **Restricted list**: substances with **maximum allowed concentration** and **use conditions**
- **Positive list of preservatives, UV filters, tar colours**: only listed substances allowed, with limits

For most "general" cosmetic ingredients, **any substance not on the prohibition list is allowed**, provided the manufacturer takes responsibility for safety. This is more permissive than the EU's purely positive lists for preservatives/UV filters/colourants.

Common founder traps:
- **Hydroquinone** banned (Annex II of the JP negative list)
- **Salicylic acid** restricted to 0.2% in cosmetics; above that = quasi-drug
- **Niacinamide** for "whitening" → quasi-drug
- **Retinol** allowed in cosmetics but anti-aging claims are tightly regulated; "anti-wrinkle" specific claims need PMDA-approved efficacy data
- **Sunscreen UV filters**: positive list, max combined concentrations

## Japanese-language labelling

Mandatory information on every cosmetic placed on the Japanese market, in **Japanese language**:

- Product name
- **Manufacturer / distributor name + address** (= MAH info, the company licensed in Japan)
- **Manufacturing site information** (country + name of factory or licensed manufacturer)
- Manufacturing number (lot)
- **Volume / weight in metric units**
- **All ingredients** in descending order of content, per **JCID names**, in **Japanese script (katakana)** for the consumer-facing list
- Date of manufacture or expiry, **if shelf life <3 years**
- **Storage warnings** if relevant
- **Country of origin** if imported

The label may include English alongside Japanese, but Japanese must be the primary language.

Different cosmetic categories have **additional category-specific labelling** — soap, hair dye, sunscreen, eye products, etc. each have extra requirements per MHLW notification.

## Claims rules

Japan strictly limits cosmetic claims. The MHLW publishes lists of **permitted cosmetic claims** (**Notification No. 14/Cosmetic** sets out 56 acceptable claim categories). Going beyond the list pushes you into quasi-drug territory.

Permitted cosmetic claims include:
- "Cleanses skin"
- "Keeps skin moisturized"
- "Refreshes skin"
- "Smoothes makeup application"
- "Suppresses dryness of skin" (with conditions)

NOT permitted in cosmetics (would require quasi-drug approval):
- "Whitens skin" / "Brightens"
- "Treats acne" / "Anti-acne"
- "Prevents hair loss" / "Hair regrowth"
- "Reduces wrinkles" (with specific quantitative claims)
- "Cellulite removal"
- Any medical/therapeutic claim

Marketing materials (web, ads, packaging) all fall under the **PMD Act Article 66 (false/exaggerated advertising)** and **Yakuji-hō Article 68** (anti-medical claims). Penalties: cease-and-desist + administrative fines + risk of import ban.

## GMP and manufacturing

For products **manufactured outside Japan**, the **manufacturing site** must be declared in the notification. While Japan doesn't legally require ISO 22716 certification of the foreign manufacturer, it is **strongly preferred** by MAHs and importers, and required by most Japanese distributors.

If you contract-manufacture in EU GMP-certified facilities, you are essentially good.

## Animal testing

Japan does not have an EU-style **animal testing ban for cosmetics**. Tests may still be required for **quasi-drug approval**. For pure cosmetics, animal-test-free declarations are common and expected by consumers, but not legally mandated.

## Customs + tariffs

Japan applies cosmetics-specific HS codes:

- **HS 3304** (beauty/skincare/makeup preparations): **0% MFN duty** for most subheadings under the WTO bound rate
- **HS 3305** (hair preparations): 0% MFN
- **HS 3401** (soap): 0% MFN
- **Consumption tax**: 10% on import value + duty

The **EU-Japan EPA** (in force Feb 2019) gives **0% preferential duty** under origin rules. For US brands, **JEUSFTA** does not specifically cover cosmetics tariffs (already at 0%) but does affect import logistics.

## Cost + timeline summary for a foreign brand entering Japan

For a typical 10-SKU skincare line, **partnership with MAH service** (most common path):

| Item | Cost (EUR equivalent) | Lead time |
|---|---|---|
| MAH service start-up + first year (10 SKUs) | €5,500–€11,000 | 4–8 weeks |
| MHLW notification × 10 SKUs | ~€1,000 (often bundled in MAH fee) | 4–8 weeks |
| Japanese label redesign + ingredient JCID mapping | €2,500–€5,000 | 3–4 weeks |
| Distributor agreement + first shipment customs | €1,500–€3,500 | 2–4 weeks |
| Stability + microbiology data (if not done) | €3,000–€6,000 | 8–12 weeks |
| Translation + linguistic review | €500–€1,500 | 1–2 weeks |
| **TOTAL Year 1** | **€13,000–€27,000** | **12–18 weeks** |

Plus **€7,000–€18,000/yr** ongoing (MAH retainer, distributor fees, post-market surveillance, renewal admin).

If you cross into **quasi-drug territory** even on one SKU, add **€18,000–€50,000 + 12–18 months** for PMDA approval per SKU.

## What can go wrong

- **Whitening claim on a cosmetic** → customs seizure or post-market enforcement
- **No MHLW notification** → import bound + product seizure, MAH licence at risk
- **Ingredient mis-translation** → product blocked; relabel + re-notify
- **Importer mismatch** with notification → customs holds; refund logistics nightmare
- **GMP audit failure** at manufacturer → MAH terminates contract, you are off the market
- **Crossed-line claim in advertising** → fines + ad takedown
